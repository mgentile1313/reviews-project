# Ground Truth Brief — Mavis Tires & Brakes (Park Rd / Dilworth), Charlotte NC

**Location ID:** cf4a2d35-2948-4073-b169-f5a3fe2733f5
**Profile:** low-performer
**Reviews read:** 249 reviews, date range 2024-05-21 to 2026-05-18
**Labeler:** Matt (with Claude as structural scribe)
**Date labeled:** 2026-05-28

---

## Top 3 actions

### 1. Stop letting appointments be theater — confirm the day before, recover when slipping

**Intervention:** Two-part change at the front desk.
(a) Day-before phone confirmation on every appointment that verifies tires/parts are in stock, a tech is assigned, and the time window is realistic. If any of those fails, reschedule the customer THEN — not when they show up. (b) When a same-day appointment slips by 30 minutes or more, front desk proactively states the situation to the customer before they ask: *"Your appointment was for X, we're running Y behind, here's what we can do."*

**Evidence:** *"Terrible service. I made an appointment the night before to get a tire plugged. I made it for 8 am. I showed up at 8 am exactly, just to be told that it is first come first served, and it would be at least three hours before they can see me. Not entirely sure what the point of an appointment is, especially for something that takes almost no time at all."* — google, 2024-11-14, 1★ (~16 other reviews echo this pattern, including the same "what's the point of an appointment" framing in 5+ of them)

**Why it matters:** Appointments are the single most cited grievance in the dataset and the easiest broken promise for a one-time customer to walk away from. The store is bleeding the new-customer top of the funnel while the regulars keep coming because they call Jim directly — that asymmetry is what makes the rating distribution look the way it does, and it will only get worse as the regular book ages out.

### 2. Verify wheel torque on every wheel-off job, signed by the tech, spot-checked by the manager

**Intervention:** Every job that takes a wheel off the car requires the tech to torque to spec with a calibrated torque wrench and initial the work order. Manager spot-checks one in five at random before vehicle release. (A 30-second pre-handoff drive-around was considered but cut — too costly in bay-time when multiplied across the day.)

**Evidence:** *"I went in for an oil change and new tires it took 6 hours and the lugnuts were not tightened on one tire when I left. The tire was so loud driving away they came out to tighten it but that should not have happened. They also did not allign the tires as stated in the service and the car does not drive straight with the steering wheel straight. These are simple tasks for a routine tire change that were not done or inspected before I got my car. This is just dangerous."* — google, 2025-11-03, 1★ (~9 other reviews describe similar post-service failures — lug nuts left loose or over-torqued, brake line not reconnected, oil cap missing, steering hardware loose post-alignment)

**Why it matters:** Lug-nut and wheel-off errors are roughly half of the post-service failure mode and are the cheapest to eliminate with a single discipline change. They also carry tort liability the store can't absorb if one of the close calls becomes a crash — the chronic pattern means the next incident is statistically owed, not freak.

### 3. Stop pressure-selling at the desk — require photo evidence + customer phone authorization for any add-on, and break out "recommended" vs "required" on every quote

**Intervention:** Two-part change. (a) Any service that adds $200 or more above the original work order requires the tech to photograph the worn part and send it to the customer's phone; customer must verbally authorize before work begins, logged on the ticket. (b) Every printed quote separates "required" from "recommended" as two distinct columns; customer signs each line independently.

**Evidence:** *"They will overcharge you and not answer questions. I brought my car in because the check engine light was on, and they replaced multiple other things without asking, charging over $4,000! Not only did they do this, but they actually worsened the issue!! We had to take it to a different Mavis, where they finally fixed it."* — google, 2025-06-17, 1★ (~7 other reviews describe the same shape: an upsell well above the original ticket, often validated by a second opinion that the work wasn't needed — e.g., $1,300 brake quote when dealer said brakes were fine, $1,800 brake pad quote, "tire can't be patched" upsells flipped at a competitor for a fraction)

**Why it matters:** The pattern is consistent enough that customers are now writing reviews specifically to warn other people to get a second opinion, which is the worst possible word-of-mouth a tire shop can have. Photo + phone authorization slows down the pressure cycle and creates an audit trail the manager can use to identify whether this is a store-wide habit or one or two specific techs.

# Ground Truth Brief — Mavis Discount Tire (Route 70), Lakewood NJ

**Location ID:** bc7fe5c3-6b80-426b-b810-36d314bce019
**Profile:** high rating with potential red flags
**Reviews read:** 278 reviews, date range 2024-05-22 to 2026-05-21
**Labeler:** Matt (with Claude as structural scribe)

---

## Top 3 actions

### 1. Lock the booking quote into a customer-takeaway contract, require signed change orders for any in-service add-on, and enforce receipt traceback at the register

**Intervention:** Three-layer change.
(a) **Printed customer-takeaway quote at booking.** Every booking generates a fully itemized quote — parts, labor, balancing, alignment, disposal, tax, and any hold/deposit fee marked refundable-or-not. Customer signs one copy and keeps it; second copy stays with the work order. The signed quote *is* the contract.
(b) **Signed change order for any in-service add-on or scope change.** Tech writes a one-line change order with price, customer signs in person or via text-back photo, and only then does the work proceed. Same applies to part substitutions.
(c) **Receipt traceback rule at the register.** Every line item on the final invoice must trace to either the original signed quote or a signed change order. Any line item that doesn't trace is removed. (A same-quote-or-lower guarantee was considered as a fourth layer but rejected — it transfers all quote-error cost onto the store unilaterally; the customer-signed change order pathway gives the same protection without that exposure. A standardized phone-quote script was also considered but rejected — corpus-divergent, hard to enforce, and the booking-stage quote captures the same value.)

**Evidence:** *"Dropped off my car at Mavis for a full set of new tires, expecting a straightforward service. Instead, I got a frustrating runaround. After 45 minutes of waiting, they called to say they 'forgot' to include the tire alignment in the quote—something their sales team apparently removes to make the price seem lower. I agreed to the alignment since they already had my car."* — google, 2025-03-13, 1★ (~3 other reviews describe the same quote-vs-invoice mechanic — a $25 "refundable hold" fee charged in the final invoice despite a phone-stated refund policy, a manager who "told me one price and then changed it after service," and one review naming the related friction: phone-quote refusal as a price-shopping prevention tactic)

**Why it matters:** Row 206's framing — *"their sales team apparently removes [alignment] to make the price seem lower"* — is the customer naming the tactic as systematic, not as a one-off mistake. That's structurally distinct from the bait-and-switch pattern at the other locations (Park Rd, Mid) where the price-mismatch reviews stayed on watchlist because there was no claim of intent. Here there is. A signed customer-takeaway quote + signed change orders + receipt traceback closes the loop at every stage of the customer journey: the booking can't omit lines, the in-service add-on can't be silent, and the register can't add unexplained charges.

### 2. Make scope and need legible at the desk and in the bay — split "required" from "recommended" on every quote with a measurement on every Recommended line, and show the customer the part before they authorize anything beyond the original ticket

**Intervention:** Three-part change, with the first two copied directly from the Mid (Jersey City) brief for cross-store consistency, and the third as a Lakewood-specific tightening.
(a) Every printed quote separates "required" from "recommended" as two distinct columns; customer signs each line independently.
(b) When a tech flags additional work above the original work order, the customer is physically walked into the bay and shown the wear, damage, or failure on their own car before they authorize.
(c) **Every "Recommended" line must include a measurement** — brake pad thickness in mm, rotor runout in mils, battery voltage and CCA, tire tread depth in 32nds. The Recommended line is data, not opinion. (Photo + customer-phone authorization on add-ons ≥$200 was considered as an additional auditable leg but rejected — same friction concern that retired it at Mid, plus the measurement-on-recommended-line is a more falsifiable control than a photo.)

**Evidence:** *"Upon checkout I was bombarded by two employees insisting that my battery was not functioning at full capacity and that my brakes and rotors were warped. I declined the service and said I would deal with it at another time. My car was not giving any indication of issues. The employees would not let it go and continued to pressure me pointing out that the car was unsafe. I still declined and took the car for a second opinion. He explained that I have AT MINIMUM 60% left to the brakes and my battery was just fine."* — google, 2026-02-23, 1★ (~3 other reviews describe the same shape — a "perfectly good" tire changed and charged for, a "we don't have your size" gambit aimed at upselling, and a two-employee tag-team pressure tactic at checkout)

**Why it matters:** Row 61 is the cleanest second-opinion-disconfirmed upsell review across all three locations labeled — the second opinion gave a *specific number* ("AT MINIMUM 60% left"). The measurement requirement on the Recommended line converts the dispute from "did the tech pressure me?" (cultural, hard to litigate, easy to spin) to "is the brake pad really 3mm?" (measurable, falsifiable, second-opinion-checkable). This is the highest-leverage controls upgrade available because it changes the *kind* of fight the customer is allowed to have with the store from subjective to objective.

### 3. Close the inter-shift and inter-week communication gaps — log every customer promise at the account level, proactively call when running behind

**Intervention:** Two-part change.
(a) **Customer-promise log at the account level.** Any promise made to a customer — courtesy credit, parts hold, callback, refund commitment, special arrangement — gets recorded as a customer-account note with timestamp and employee initials. **Primary path: enter the note in the corporate POS/CRM** so it's visible across visits, across employees, and across stores. **Fallback path: if the POS doesn't support customer-account notes, a store-level logbook at the front desk with daily manager review of unresolved entries.** (A daily verbal shift-change handoff was considered as a lighter-weight alternative but rejected — the credit-doesn't-exist case below is a multi-week continuity gap that survives any same-day ritual; only an account-level record carries across that horizon.)
(b) **30-minute slip rule.** When any appointment will run more than 30 minutes past the start time, front desk proactively calls the customer before they ask, with three options: wait, reschedule, or leave the car for later pickup. (Direct copy of Park Rd #1(b) for cross-store consistency.)

**Evidence:** *"Had a issue back in March I was told they will give me curtsey credit and it will be saved under my phone number for if and when i would like to return. Wife went today to get oil change and being told credit doesn't exist."* — google, 2025-10-20, 2★ (~3 other reviews echo the within-store-communication breakdown — a customer not notified about a parts/scope issue until after arrival, a mechanic arriving 1.5-2 hours late with no proactive call, and one review naming the divide explicitly: *"VERY poor communication. To customers, and fellow employees."*)
